Speech Title:

Grit: The Strongest Predictor of Success

Speaker:

Angela Duckworth

Summary of the Video:

   When Angela was 27 years old she left a very demanding job in management consulting for even more demanding job: teaching.

     She went to teach seventh graders math in New York public schools. Like any other teachers she made quizzes and test, gave out homework assignments. When the students work came back she calculated the grades. What struck her the most was that IQ was not the only difference between the best and worst students. Some of the strongest performers did not have stratospheric IQ scores. Some of the smartest kids weren’t doing so well. And it got her thinking, the things you need to learn in seventh grade math is hard but it’s not impossible. And she was convinced that every student could learn it if they work hard. After several years of teaching, she came to the conclusion that what we need in education is a much better understanding of students and learning from a motivational perspective. In education, the one thing we know, is to measure best is IQ. But what if doing well in school and life depends on much more than your ability to learn quickly and easily? So she went to graduate school to become a psychologist. Start studying kids and adults in all kinds of challenging settings, in every study her question was, “Who is successful here and why?” Her and her research team went to West Point Military Academy. Tries to predict who would stay in military training and which would drop out. Went to national spelling bee, tries to predict which student advance farthest in competition. Studied rookie teachers and working really tough neighborhoods. One characteristic emerged as a significant predictor of success. Grit, grit is passion and perseverance for very long-term goals. It is having stamina, sticking with your future, day in, day out, not just for the week, not for just the month, but for years, and working really hard to make that future a reality. She started studying grit in Chicago public schools. Ask thousands of high school juniors to take grit questionnaires, and then waited around more than a year to see who would graduate. The grittier kids were significantly more likely to graduate.

     We need to take our best ideas, our strongest intuitions, and we need to test them. We need to measure whether we’ve been successful and we have to be willing to fail, to be wrong, to start over again with lessons learned. In other word we need to be gritty about getting kids grittier.
